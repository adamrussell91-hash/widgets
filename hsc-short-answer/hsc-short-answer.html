Predict how you'll go
Why are you doing this?
Research on how students actually improve shows one thing clearly: the students who get better fastest are not the ones who write the most answers — they're the ones who know what they don't know. Predicting your performance before you write forces you to confront that honestly.

After you self-mark, you'll see exactly where your prediction matched reality and where it didn't. That gap — between what you thought you'd do and what you actually did — is the most precise signal possible about where to direct your practice. Without this step, you can spend hours practising the wrong things and never know it.

So: be brutally honest. No one sees this. "Yes" means you're genuinely confident you'll hit this. "No" means you know you won't. "Unsure" means you genuinely don't know. Saying yes to everything defeats the purpose entirely.
